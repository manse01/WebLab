About Me

I went to undergrad at the University of Miami, and obtained my Bachelors degree in Psychology. I became fascinated with this field because of the plasticity of the human mind. What really solidified my interest in the field of Industrial Organizational Psychology, was when I took classes in Positive Psychology and I/O Psychology. I throughly enjoyed the lighter side of Psychology depicted in Positive Psychology, something that I never knew was an actual subset of the field. I liked the notion of constant self-improvement, increased life satisfaction, and long-term growth. When taking the I/O class in conjunction with the positive psych class I could see how these two aspects of psychology are interwoven with one another. Being that adults spend most of their lives working, it made sense to me that the working environment should be a place that fosters innovation, optimism, and allows workers to be engaged and realize their full potentials.

For as long as I can remember, I have always possessed an innate ability to skillfully advise and counsel those in my life, whether these individuals sought me out for guidance with romantic relationships, family issues, or even solely emotional support.  I would actually instruct all of my friends to come to my home one at a time after school, and have them meet with me, so that I could analyze and assess their situations individually, and provide them with constructive feedback. I took on this role not only because they were my friends and very dear to me, but because dissecting a situation piece-by-piece, and examining the cognitive irrationalities stemming from an emotional bias towards a situation fascinated me. The consistency with which humans perpetuate their own follies astounds me. I began to recognize a pattern that was ubiquitous regardless of which friend I spoke with; the unyielding hold that our own cognitions and neural mechanisms have on our decisions is truly a double-edged sword. In one sense, when we make unsatisfactory choices in particular past situations, our memories from those experiences serve to effectively influence how we respond the next time to a similar situation (e.g. to make a better choice the second time around). Unfortunately, in other specific situations, our poor decisions the first time fail to have a significant effect on our decision-making even the fourth and fifth time around!

I am astonished by this notion that when put in particular congruent scenarios again and again, our reasoning is almost inflexible because of the strong neural habit loops upon which our brains have created an insatiable dependence. This deduction set me on a mission to try to unravel factors that lead to the promotion of self-improvement, personal growth, self-efficacy, self-satisfaction and optimistic thinking. The endeavor to not be complacent and always strive to become a better version of oneself is a valiant quest, that can be attained though the application of the theories present in I/O Psychology.
